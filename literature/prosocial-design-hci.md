## 1. Foundational Frameworks

### 1.1 Constitutive vs. Regulative Rules (Philosophical Foundation)

**Searle, J. R. (2018). Constitutive Rules. *Argumenta*, 3(1), 51–54.**  
DOI: 10.14275/2465-2334/20183.sea  
Key contribution: Formalizes the distinction between regulative rules (which regulate pre-existing behavior) and constitutive rules (which create new forms of behavior and institutional facts). The "X counts as Y in context C" formula is foundational.  
Relevance: **Core theoretical framework** for Protocol Love. The paper argues that prosociality can be *constituted* at the protocol level rather than merely *regulated* after the fact.

**Searle, J. R. (1995). *The Construction of Social Reality*. New York: Free Press.**  
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Construction_of_Social_Reality  
Key contribution: Introduces status functions, collective intentionality, and institutional facts as the building blocks of social reality. Explains how constitutive rules create deontic powers.  
Relevance: Provides the ontological grounding for understanding how digital protocols create new social realities with embedded prosocial affordances.

**Searle, J. R. (2015). Status Functions and Institutional Facts: Reply to Hindriks and Guala. *Journal of Institutional Economics*, 11(3), 507–514.**  
DOI: 10.1017/S1744137414000629  
Key contribution: Defends the constitutive rule approach against reduction to regulative rules or game-theoretic equilibria. Argues only constitutive rules can properly account for institutional reality.  
Relevance: Strengthens the theoretical argument that protocol-level design (constitutive) is fundamentally different from moderation-based approaches (regulative).

### 1.2 Code as Law / Architecture as Regulation

**Lessig, L. (1999). *Code and Other Laws of Cyberspace*. New York: Basic Books.**  
URL: https://lessig.org/product/codev2/  
Key contribution: Argues that code/architecture is one of four modalities of regulation (alongside law, norms, and markets). "Code is law" — the architecture of digital systems shapes behavior as powerfully as legal regulation.  
Relevance: **Directly supports** the Protocol Love thesis that design choices at the protocol level are constitutive acts that shape social behavior. Lessig's four modalities map onto the paper's framework of protocol-level prosocial design.

**Lessig, L. (2000). Code Is Law: On Liberty in Cyberspace. *Harvard Magazine*, January 2000.**  
URL: https://harvardmagazine.com/2000/01/code-is-law-html  
Key contribution: Accessible articulation of the "code is law" thesis — that the software and hardware of cyberspace set the terms of social life within it.  
Relevance: Establishes the precedent that protocol/architecture-level design is a form of governance, not merely technical implementation.

### 1.3 Values in Design

**Flanagan, M. & Nissenbaum, H. (2014). *Values at Play in Digital Games*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.**  
DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9788.001.0001  
Key contribution: Presents Values at Play (VAP), a theoretical and practical framework for identifying and embedding moral/political values (justice, equity, cooperation) in digital games. Introduces a systematic heuristic for values-conscious game design.  
Relevance: Provides methodological precedent for intentionally embedding prosocial values in game/system design. The VAP framework complements Protocol Love's constitutive approach by showing how values can be designed-in rather than enforced after the fact.

**Flanagan, M., Howe, D. C., & Nissenbaum, H. (2005). Embodying Values in Technology: Theory and Practice. In *Information Technology and Moral Philosophy*, Cambridge University Press.**  
URL: https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/Nissenbaum-VID.4-25.pdf  
Key contribution: Argues that values are inherently embodied in technical artifacts and proposes design methodologies (including VAP) for intentionally embedding positive values.  
Relevance: Theoretical support for the claim that protocols are never value-neutral — they always embody values, and designers should do so intentionally and prosocially.

**Friedman, B. & Hendry, D. G. (2019). *Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.**  
DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/7585.001.0001  
Key contribution: Comprehensive treatment of Value Sensitive Design (VSD), a tripartite methodology (conceptual, empirical, technical investigations) for incorporating human values into technology design.  
Relevance: VSD provides an established methodological framework that Protocol Love can position against — arguing for a more constitutive (rather than investigative) approach to values in protocol design.

## 2. Prosocial Design Frameworks

### 2.1 Prosocial Design (General)

**Grüning, D., Kamin, J., Panizza, F., et al. (2024). A Framework for Promoting Online Prosocial Behavior via Digital Interventions. *Communications Psychology*, 2, Article 52.**  
DOI: 10.1038/s44271-023-00052-7  
Key contribution: Categorizes digital interventions for prosocial behavior into three types based on timing: proactive (before behavior), interactive (during behavior), and reactive (after behavior). Reviews evidence for each category.  
Relevance: **Highly relevant** — this framework maps directly onto Protocol Love's distinction between constitutive (proactive, built-in) and regulative (reactive, moderation-based) approaches. Protocol Love extends this by arguing that the most powerful interventions are constitutive/architectural, not just temporally proactive.

**Grüning, D. & Kamin, J. (2025). Prosocial Design in Trust and Safety. *arXiv preprint*, arXiv:2506.12792.**  
URL: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.12792  
Key contribution: Defines Prosocial Design as "the set of design patterns, features and processes which foster healthy interactions." Articulates core principles: dignity of the user, non-neutral design, and proaction.  
Relevance: Establishes prosocial design as a recognized approach in platform governance. Protocol Love can build on and extend this by adding the constitutive/regulative distinction.

**Prosocial Design Network. (n.d.). Browse Interventions.**  
URL: https://www.prosocialdesign.org/  
Key contribution: Curates a library of evidence-based prosocial design interventions (e.g., social reference cues, commitment pledges, post guidance, shared humanity memes) with ratings of effectiveness.  
Relevance: Provides a practical catalogue of prosocial design patterns that Protocol Love can analyze through the constitutive vs. regulative lens.

**Schirch, L., Slachmuijlder, L., & Iyer, R. (2023). Toward Prosocial Tech Design Governance. *TechPolicy.Press*.**  
URL: https://techpolicy.press/toward-prosocial-tech-design-governance  
Key contribution: Argues for shifting from content governance (reactive) to design governance (proactive). Proposes prosocial tech design as a framework for strengthening social cohesion through platform architecture.  
Relevance: Directly aligned with Protocol Love's thesis — both argue for upstream, architectural intervention rather than downstream content moderation.

**Schirch, L. et al. (2025). Blueprint on Prosocial Tech Design Governance. Council on Technology and Social Cohesion / University of Notre Dame.**  
URL: https://keough.nd.edu/assets/619669/blueprint_on_prosocial_tech_design_governance_aa.pdf  
Key contribution: Actionable policy recommendations for governments and platforms organized in three tiers of prosocial design governance. Based on eight years of research and 12 workshops.  
Relevance: Provides the governance/policy dimension of prosocial design that Protocol Love's constitutive framework could inform.

### 2.2 Positive Computing & Wellbeing

**Calvo, R. A. & Peters, D. (2014). *Positive Computing: Technology for Wellbeing and Human Potential*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.**  
DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9764.001.0001  
Key contribution: Foundational text defining "positive computing" — the design and development of technology to support psychological wellbeing. Examines wellbeing factors including positive emotions, mindfulness, empathy, compassion, and altruism.  
Relevance: Establishes the broader design paradigm within which Protocol Love operates. Positive computing provides the wellbeing-oriented framing; Protocol Love adds the constitutive mechanism for achieving it.

**Peters, D. & Calvo, R. A. (2014). Compassion vs. Empathy: Designing for Resilience. *Interactions*, 21(5), 48–53.**  
DOI: 10.1145/2647087  
Key contribution: Distinguishes between empathy and compassion in technology design, arguing compassion (action-oriented concern) is more sustainable and designable than empathy (emotional mirroring).  
Relevance: Informs Protocol Love's care ethics grounding — constitutive protocol design should foster compassion (sustained prosocial action) rather than mere empathic identification.

**Kim, J. et al. (2024). Design Patterns for the Common Good: Building Better Technologies Using the Wisdom of Virtue Ethics. In *Proceedings of CHI '25*, Yokohama, Japan. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3706598.3713546  
Key contribution: Proposes a systematic approach inspired by virtue ethics for identifying common-good-oriented design patterns (Positech paradigm). Argues for building technologies that foster flourishing rather than merely fixing harms.  
Relevance: Complementary philosophical framework — virtue ethics as a design paradigm parallels Protocol Love's care ethics approach. Both argue for constitutive goodness rather than reactive harm reduction.

### 2.3 Persuasive Technology & Nudging

**Fogg, B. J. (2009). A Behavior Model for Persuasive Design. In *Proceedings of Persuasive '09*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/1541948.1541999  
Key contribution: Introduces the Fogg Behavior Model (FBM): behavior = motivation × ability × trigger. Foundational framework for persuasive technology design.  
Relevance: Protocol Love can position against persuasive technology — arguing that constitutive design differs from persuasion. Constitutive rules don't persuade existing behavior; they create new forms of behavior.

**Caraban, A., Karapanos, E., Gonçalves, D., & Campos, P. (2019). 23 Ways to Nudge: A Review of Technology-Mediated Nudging in Human-Computer Interaction. In *Proceedings of CHI '19*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3290605.3300733  
Key contribution: Systematic review identifying 23 distinct nudging mechanisms in 6 categories, leveraging 15 cognitive biases. Lays out the design space of technology-mediated nudging.  
Relevance: Nudging operates at the regulative level (altering choice architecture for existing behaviors). Protocol Love's constitutive approach is categorically different — creating new behavioral possibilities rather than nudging within existing ones.

## 3. Prosocial Game Design

### 3.1 Kind Games & Prosocial Mechanics

**Cook, D., Lau, J. C., Tan, M. L., Burgess, J., Moriwaki, T., & Kajioka, E. D. (2022). Kind Games: Designing for Prosocial Multiplayer. *Polaris Game Design Retreat*.**  
URL: https://polarisgamedesign.com/2022/kind-games-designing-for-prosocial-multiplayer/  
Key contribution: Defines "kind games" as multiplayer games designed from the start with systems that deliberately promote prosocial behavior. Identifies key design patterns: safety, interdependence, shared goals, limited communication. Analyzes Journey, Sky, Death Stranding, Elden Ring.  
Relevance: **Directly relevant case study source.** The kind games framework is essentially a practitioner articulation of constitutive prosocial design — designing the rules/systems to *make* kindness the natural behavior, not policing unkindness.

**Cook, D. (2020). Prosocial Economics for Game Design. *Project Horseshoe / Lostgarden*.**  
URL: https://lostgarden.com/2020/01/11/prosocial-economics-for-game-design/  
Key contribution: Focuses on economic design patterns that support prosocial systems: positive-sum resources, friendship formation economics, trust-building through cooperative success. Identifies dark patterns that sabotage prosociality.  
Relevance: Demonstrates how *economic* protocol design (currencies, resource systems) constitutively shapes prosocial or antisocial behavior — directly supporting Protocol Love's argument about constitutive design through currencies and interaction constraints.

### 3.2 Journey & thatgamecompany

**Chen, J. (2013). Designing Journey. *Game Developers Conference (GDC) 2013*.**  
URL: https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1017700/Designing  
Key contribution: Jenova Chen's design philosophy for Journey: removing identity markers, limiting communication to wordless chirps, anonymous matchmaking — all to create emotional connection between strangers. Design goal was trust-building and awe, not competition.  
Relevance: **Primary case study.** Journey is the quintessential example of constitutive prosocial protocol design — the rules of the game *constitute* a world where prosociality emerges naturally because competitive/toxic interactions are structurally impossible.

**Bell, C. (2012). Designing for Friendship: The Art of Journey's Multiplayer. *Game Developers Conference (GDC) 2012*.**  
URL: https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1015524/Designing-for-Friendship-The-Art  
Key contribution: Details Journey's multiplayer design decisions: no lobby, no names, no voice chat, limited vocabulary (chirps only). Players choose to cooperate because the system constitutes a world where cooperation feels natural.  
Relevance: Technical design details showing how constitutive rules (no identity, no language, anonymous pairing) create prosocial outcomes without any moderation or regulation.

**He, X. (2023). Deep Dive: Altruism in Online Multiplayer Video Games with Kind Words and Sky: Children of the Light. *Game Developer*.**  
URL: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/deep-dive-altruism-in-online-multiplayer-video-games-with-kind-words-and-sky-children-of-the-light  
Key contribution: Analyzes altruistic behavior in Kind Words and Sky as products of intentional design. In Kind Words, pure altruism emerges because there is no reciprocity motivation or reputation system. In Sky, empathy drives helping because the game creates vulnerability and interdependence.  
Relevance: Demonstrates how different constitutive designs produce different forms of prosociality (pure altruism vs. empathy-driven cooperation) — supporting Protocol Love's framework of constitutive design dimensions.

### 3.3 Dark Souls Asynchronous Messages

**FromSoftware. (2011–2023). Dark Souls series — Orange Guidance Soapstone message system.**  
URL: https://darksouls.fandom.com/wiki/Messages  
Key contribution: Players communicate across worlds using pre-constructed messages from a template of predetermined phrases. Messages can be rated. This constrained vocabulary system produces a distinctive prosocial/playful communication culture despite the game's punishing difficulty.  
Relevance: **Key case study** for Protocol Love. The soapstone message system is a constitutive protocol: by constraining communication to a template vocabulary, it *constitutes* a unique form of asynchronous cooperation (and playful deception) that could not exist without these exact rules.

### 3.4 Empirical Evidence: Prosocial Game Effects

**Greitemeyer, T. & Mügge, D. O. (2014). Video Games Do Affect Social Outcomes: A Meta-Analytic Review of the Effects of Violent and Prosocial Video Game Play. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 40(5), 578–589.**  
DOI: 10.1177/0146167213520459  
Key contribution: Meta-analysis of 98 studies (N=36,965): prosocial video games significantly increase prosocial behavior, empathy, and prosocial cognition while decreasing aggression. Effect size r = .20 for prosocial outcomes.  
Relevance: Provides empirical grounding that game content/design affects prosocial behavior — supporting the claim that constitutive protocol design has real behavioral effects.

**Gentile, D. A., Anderson, C. A., Yukawa, S., et al. (2009). The Effects of Prosocial Video Games on Prosocial Behaviors: International Evidence from Correlational, Longitudinal, and Experimental Studies. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 35(6), 752–763.**  
DOI: 10.1177/0146167209333045  
Key contribution: Three-study program (correlational, longitudinal, experimental) providing international evidence that prosocial video game exposure increases helping, cooperation, sharing, and empathy. Based on the General Learning Model.  
Relevance: Establishes the causal mechanism — prosocial game content leads to prosocial behavior through cognitive and affective pathways. Validates the premise that how games are designed (their constitutive rules) shapes player prosociality.

**Greitemeyer, T. & Osswald, S. (2010). Effects of Prosocial Video Games on Prosocial Behavior. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 98(2), 211–221.**  
DOI: 10.1037/a0016997  
Key contribution: Experimental evidence that playing prosocial (vs. neutral) video games increases helping behavior, mediated by increased accessibility of prosocial thoughts and empathy.  
Relevance: Demonstrates the mediating mechanisms through which game design (constitutive rules) affects prosocial behavior.

**Greitemeyer, T., Osswald, S., & Brauer, M. (2010). Playing Prosocial Video Games Increases Empathy and Decreases Schadenfreude. *Emotion*, 10(6), 796–802.**  
DOI: 10.1037/a0020194  
Key contribution: Prosocial game exposure increases empathy and decreases pleasure at others' misfortune (schadenfreude).  
Relevance: Shows that constitutive game design affects not just behavior but emotional orientation — relevant to Protocol Love's care ethics framing.

**Greitemeyer, T. (2013). Playing Video Games Cooperatively Increases Empathic Concern. *Social Psychology*, 44(6), 408–413.**  
DOI: 10.1027/1864-9335/a000154  
Key contribution: Cooperative (vs. competitive) game play specifically increases empathic concern. The *mode* of play matters, not just content.  
Relevance: Supports Protocol Love's argument that the structural/constitutive design of interaction (cooperative vs. competitive) matters more than surface content.

### 3.5 Cooperative Game Design Theory

**Riar, M., Morschheuser, B., Hamari, J., & Zarnekow, R. (2023). Altruism or Egoism — How Do Game Features Motivate Cooperation? An Investigation into User We-Intention and I-Intention. *Behaviour & Information Technology*, 43(7), 1355–1378.**  
DOI: 10.1080/0144929X.2023.2196581  
Key contribution: Survey of Ingress players (N=206) showing that cooperative game features give rise to within-group altruism (enjoyment in helping, cooperative goals), which forms we-intentions. Individualistic features invoke I-intentions and self-centered motivations.  
Relevance: Empirical evidence that specific game design features (constitutive rules) produce altruistic vs. egoistic orientations — supporting Protocol Love's claim about constitutive design shaping prosocial orientations.

**Steinemann, S. T., Geelan, B., de Salas, K., & Opwis, K. (2017). Simple Acts for a Better World: A Gameful System for Prosocial Behavior. In *Proceedings of CHI PLAY '17 (Companion)*, pp. 305–313. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3130859.3131295  
Key contribution: Preliminary design and research plan for a gameful system that encourages real-world prosocial behavior through game mechanics.  
Relevance: Example of applying game design principles to promote prosocial behavior beyond the game context — extending constitutive design thinking to real-world systems.

**Apostolakis, K. C., Kaza, K., Psaltis, A., et al. (2017). Path of Trust: A Prosocial Co-Op Game for Building up Trustworthiness and Teamwork. In *Serious Games: Third Joint International Conference*. Springer.**  
DOI: 10.1007/978-3-319-70111-0_6  
Key contribution: Digital cooperative game designed to teach prosocial skills (cooperation, trustworthiness) to children aged 7–10 by redefining traditional game mechanics toward prosocial goals.  
Relevance: Example of constitutive game design for prosocial outcomes — the game's rules are designed to *constitute* cooperative behavior as the only path to success.

**Parsons, S., Karakosta, E., Boniface, M., & Crowle, S. (2019). Prosocial Games for Inclusion: Interaction Patterns and Game Outcomes for Elementary-Aged Children. *International Journal of Child-Computer Interaction*, 22, Article 100142.**  
DOI: 10.1016/j.ijcci.2019.100142  
Key contribution: Evaluates a "shared goal" cooperative digital game (The Chase) where children must cooperate to succeed. Even in very short play periods, children shifted toward cooperative play.  
Relevance: Demonstrates that constitutive design (shared-goal interaction patterns) rapidly produces prosocial behavioral change — games that *require* cooperation constitute cooperative behavior.

## 4. Prosocial Technology & Digital Interventions

### 4.1 Prosocial Behavior via Technology

**Shin, S. Y., Liu, W., Stine, J. G., Dey, A. K., & Rho, E. H. R. (2023). "We need to do more ... I need to do more": Augmenting Digital Media Consumption via Critical Reflection to Increase Compassion and Promote Prosocial Attitudes and Behaviors. In *Proceedings of CHI '23*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3544548.3581355  
Key contribution: Tests critical reflection interventions during digital media consumption to promote compassion and prosocial attitudes. Emotion-based and cognition-based reflection both promote prosocial change.  
Relevance: Demonstrates that design interventions at the point of media consumption can promote prosociality — a form of interactive (constitutive at the experience level) design for prosocial outcomes.

**McKee, K. R., Tacchetti, A., Bakker, M. A., et al. (2023). Scaffolding Cooperation in Human Groups with Deep Reinforcement Learning. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 7, 1787–1796.**  
DOI: 10.1038/s41562-023-01686-7  
Key contribution: Uses deep RL to train a "social planner" that structures networks of human participants to maximize cooperation. The AI learns a conciliatory approach — rewarding defectors with cooperative partners rather than punishing them.  
Relevance: Demonstrates AI-mediated constitutive design for prosociality: the social planner creates network structures (constitutive rules) that make cooperation emerge naturally, rather than punishing defection (regulative approach).

**Santos, F. P. (2024). Prosocial Dynamics in Multiagent Systems. *AI Magazine*, 45(1), 131–138.**  
DOI: 10.1002/aaai.12143  
Key contribution: Analyzes prosociality across five domains (reputation systems, recommender systems, hybrid systems, classification systems, urban systems) using tools from AI, multiagent systems, and population dynamics.  
Relevance: Shows how system design (constitutive architecture) across multiple domains shapes prosocial dynamics — extending the constitutive design argument beyond games to broader sociotechnical systems.

### 4.2 Human-Robot Prosocial Interaction

**Chi, V. B. et al. (2024). Should I Help a Delivery Robot? Cultivating Prosocial Norms through Observations. In *Proceedings of CHI EA '24*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3613905.3650855  
Key contribution: Proposes using social observation and conformity to cultivate prosocial norms toward delivery robots. Familiarity and prosocial observation increase feelings of obligation to help.  
Relevance: Demonstrates how designed observation protocols (constitutive experiences) can create new prosocial norms — extending the constitutive design principle to human-robot interaction.

### 4.3 XR and Embodied Prosocial Design

**Frontiers Editorial. (2024). Advancing Prosociality in Extended Reality: Systematic Review of the Use of Embodied Virtual Agents to Trigger Prosocial Behaviour. *Frontiers in Virtual Reality*, 5, Article 1386460.**  
DOI: 10.3389/frvir.2024.1386460  
Key contribution: Systematic review of how embodied virtual agents in XR can trigger prosocial behavior. Identifies that the design of interactive environments can encourage prosocial inclinations.  
Relevance: Extends the constitutive prosocial design argument into XR — the architecture of virtual environments constitutes certain prosocial possibilities.

## 5. Prosocial Computing & Community Design

### 5.1 UW Prosocial Computing Group

**UW Prosocial Computing Group. (n.d.). Research Overview.**  
URL: https://prosocialcomputing.com/  
Key contribution: Research group at University of Washington studying technologies that foster prosocial behaviors. Uses theories from psychology, communication, and economics to inform design. Publications at CHI '23, '24, CSCW '22.  
Relevance: Active research community producing design guidelines and tools for prosocial technology — represents the state of the art in prosocial HCI research.

### 5.2 Suffering-Centered Design

**Prabhakar, A. S. (2020). Suffering-Centered Design. In *Extended Abstracts of CHI '20*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3334480.3381812  
Key contribution: Proposes that HCI should engage with suffering as a design lens — opening people's minds to others' suffering as a route to prosocial engagement. Complements positive computing with attention to negative experience.  
Relevance: Offers a counterpoint/complement to positive-computing-based prosocial design — Protocol Love's care ethics grounding similarly attends to vulnerability and suffering as motivations for constitutive prosocial design.

## 6. Constitutive Rules in Digital/Virtual Contexts

**Górski, M. (2022). How Do Base Rules Constitute a Virtual World? *International Journal for the Semiotics of Law*, 36, 1217–1239.**  
DOI: 10.1007/s11196-022-09903-8  
Key contribution: Applies Searle's institutional theory to virtual worlds, analyzing how constitutive rules create the framework for social interaction in digital spaces. Addresses the question of whether rules "translate" from analog to digital contexts.  
Relevance: **Directly relevant** — provides the theoretical bridge between Searle's constitutive rules and digital protocol design. Shows that constitutive rules operate in virtual environments just as in physical institutions.

**Terravecchia, G. P. (2020). Constitutive Rule, Regulative Rule, Emergent Rule, Normativity. *Phenomenology and Mind*, 18.**  
DOI: 10.13128/pam-8400  
Key contribution: Extends Searle's taxonomy by introducing "emergent rules" — norms that arise from practice without being constitutive or regulative. Argues Searle's framework needs this addition to account for complex social reality.  
Relevance: The concept of emergent rules is important for Protocol Love — some prosocial norms in games like Journey emerge from constitutive design without being explicitly designed, representing emergent prosociality.

## 7. Care Ethics & Technology

**Prabhakar, A. S. (2018). Designing for Compassion Cultivation. In *Extended Abstracts of CHI '18*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/3170427.3180302  
Key contribution: Proposes design strategies for cultivating compassion through technology, grounded in contemplative practices and care ethics.  
Relevance: Connects care ethics (a core philosophical pillar of Protocol Love) to HCI design practice.

**Wright, P. & McCarthy, J. (2008). Empathy and Experience in HCI. In *Proceedings of CHI '08*. ACM.**  
DOI: 10.1145/1357054.1357156  
Key contribution: Argues for empathy as a central concern in HCI, connecting emotional experience to design methodology.  
Relevance: Foundational HCI work on empathy that Protocol Love builds upon — the paper extends empathy-in-design from an analytical tool to a constitutive design goal.

## 8. Death Stranding & Strand Games

**Kojima, H. (2019). Death Stranding — "Strand Game" genre.**  
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_Stranding  
Key contribution: Hideo Kojima coined "strand game" to describe Death Stranding's core mechanic: players in separate worlds contribute infrastructure (bridges, roads, shelters) that appears in other players' worlds. Asynchronous cooperation without direct interaction.  
Relevance: **Primary case study** for Protocol Love. Death Stranding's "strand" system is a constitutive protocol — the game's rules create a world where every player action can benefit strangers. Prosociality is *constituted* by the protocol, not enforced by moderation.

## 9. Kind Words & Anonymous Kindness

**Popcannibal. (2019). Kind Words (lo fi chill beats to write to).**  
URL: https://store.steampowered.com/app/1070710/Kind_Words_lo_fi_chill_beats_to_write_to/  
Key contribution: A game where players write anonymous letters of kindness to strangers who share their worries. No usernames, no profiles, no reciprocity tracking. Pure altruism emerges from the constitutive design.  
Relevance: **Primary case study** for Protocol Love. Kind Words demonstrates that when a protocol constitutively removes identity, competition, and reciprocity tracking, pure altruism naturally emerges. The protocol *is* the prosocial intervention.
