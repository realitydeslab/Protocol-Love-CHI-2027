# Literature Review: Game Design for Prosociality and Cooperation

**Paper:** Protocol Love: Constitutive Protocol Design for Prosocial Worlding  
**Author:** Botao Amber Hu  
**Subdomain:** Prosocial Game Design, Cooperative Mechanics, Emotional Design

---

## Seminal Works

### 1. Gentile, D. A., Anderson, C. A., Yukawa, S., Ihori, N., Saleem, M., Ming, L. K., ... & Sakamoto, A. (2009). The Effects of Prosocial Video Games on Prosocial Behaviors: International Evidence from Correlational, Longitudinal, and Experimental Studies. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 35(6), 752–763. DOI: 10.1177/0146167209333045

**Key contribution:** Landmark three-country study establishing that prosocial game content increases helping, cooperation, sharing, and empathy across correlational, longitudinal, and experimental methods. Introduces five dimensions (amount, content, context, structure, mechanics) through which games affect behavior.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides the empirical foundation that game content—particularly cooperative and helping mechanics—shapes prosocial behavior, validating the premise that protocol-level design can constitutively produce prosociality.

### 2. Greitemeyer, T., & Osswald, S. (2010). Effects of Prosocial Video Games on Prosocial Behavior. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 98(2), 211–221. DOI: 10.1037/a0016997

**Key contribution:** Four experiments demonstrating that prosocial game play increases helping behavior through cognitive activation of prosocial thoughts (not just affect). Players of prosocial games were more likely to help after mishaps, volunteer for experiments, and intervene in harassment.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates the cognitive pathway through which game mechanics activate prosocial schemas—supporting the argument that protocols (not just narratives) shape social cognition.

### 3. Greitemeyer, T., Osswald, S., & Brauer, M. (2010). Playing Prosocial Video Games Increases Empathy and Decreases Schadenfreude. *Emotion*, 10(6), 796–802. DOI: 10.1037/a0020194

**Key contribution:** Extends the General Learning Model to affective outcomes: prosocial games increase interpersonal empathy and decrease pleasure at others' misfortune (schadenfreude).  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Supports the claim that designed interaction protocols can reshape emotional dispositions toward others, not just behavioral outputs.

### 4. Greitemeyer, T. (2011). Effects of Prosocial Media on Social Behavior: When and Why Does Media Exposure Affect Helping and Aggression? *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 20(4), 251–255. DOI: 10.1177/0963721411415229

**Key contribution:** Comprehensive review synthesizing evidence that prosocial media (games and music) increases prosocial thoughts, empathy, and helping while decreasing aggression. Provides an integrative framework via the General Learning Model.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes theoretical scaffolding (GLM) for understanding how designed media experiences—including game protocols—shape prosocial vs. antisocial behavioral tendencies.

## Journey (thatgamecompany)

### 5. Chen, J. (2013). Designing Journey. *GDC 2013 Talk*. URL: https://gdcvault.com/play/1017700/Designing

**Key contribution:** Jenova Chen's definitive design postmortem. 75% of development time was R&D to make players emotionally connect. Removed names, text chat, competitive elements; rewarded cooperative behavior with audiovisual feedback while minimizing griefing feedback. 12 prototypes tested before final form.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** The quintessential case study of constitutive protocol design—stripping communication to minimal signals and designing mechanics that make cooperation the emergent, natural behavior. Protocol constraints produce prosociality.

### 6. Bell, C. (2012). Designing for Friendship: Shaping Player Relationships with Rules and Freedom. *GDC 2012 Talk*. URL: https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1016117/Designing-for-Friendship-Shaping-Player

**Key contribution:** Journey designer Chris Bell's framework for designing friendship between anonymous strangers online. Emphasizes shared goals, anonymity, vulnerability, non-verbal communication, and getting people to empathize "before their prejudices allow them to draw lines."  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Directly theorizes how protocol constraints (anonymity, limited communication, shared vulnerability) constitute conditions for friendship formation. A practitioner's theory of constitutive prosocial design.

### 7. Clark, N. (2014). Cutting Corners: Networking Design in Journey. *Game Developer*. URL: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/cutting-corners-networking-design-in-journey

**Key contribution:** Technical deep-dive into Journey's networking design. Five goals: online indistinguishable from offline, smoothness over accuracy, minimize griefing, connect as many players as possible, minimize engineering effort. Reveals how technical protocol decisions (peer-to-peer, avatar push-away) shaped social experience.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Shows how low-level network protocol decisions constitutively shape social interaction patterns. Technical infrastructure as prosocial design.

### 8. Chen, J. (2014). Social Games Should Be About an Exchange of Emotions, Not Numbers. *Games for Change / Polygon*. URL: https://www.polygon.com/2014/4/25/5653356/jenova-chen-social-games-emotions-journey

**Key contribution:** Chen argues "social means emotional exchange, not number exchange." Critiques FarmVille-style social games. Describes Journey's design philosophy: removing usernames, friend lists, and chat to force emotional connection through action alone.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Articulates the distinction between instrumental social mechanics (number exchange) and constitutive social mechanics (emotional exchange)—central to Protocol Love's thesis.

### 9. Murray, J. H. (2013). Games and the Pleasure of Synchronized Behavior. *Inventing the Medium* (blog). URL: https://inventingthemedium.com/2013/04/08/games-and-the-pleasure-of-synchronized-behavior/

**Key contribution:** Janet Murray analyzes Journey's multiplayer as evoking "the synchronization of behavior with another human being, the feeling of overcoming the isolation of one's own consciousness." Connects game pleasure to fundamental human desire for connection.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Theoretical framing of how protocol-constrained interaction produces experiences of synchrony and transcendence—the "love" in Protocol Love.

### 10. Bülgözdi, I. (2023). Wordless World: The Circulation of Affects in Journey. *Acta Ludologica*. URL: https://journals.indexcopernicus.com/api/file/viewByFileId/2304881

**Key contribution:** Explores Journey's communicative possibilities without verbal language. Analyzes the circulation of affects between creators, players, and fan community. Examines how the game's decade-long legacy sustains emotional connections.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates how protocol constraints (wordlessness) create affective circulation that extends beyond the game into community practice—prosocial worlding in action.

## Sky: Children of the Light (thatgamecompany)

### 11. Ferrara, E., et al. (2022). The Gift that Keeps on Giving: Generosity is Contagious in Multiplayer Online Games. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction (CSCW)*, 6(CSCW2). DOI: 10.1145/3555120

**Key contribution:** Large-scale empirical study (~1 million players) of generosity contagion in Sky: Children of the Light. Finds that experiencing generosity (candle gifting) increases subsequent engagement and generous behavior. Provides evidence of social contagion in prosocial game mechanics.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Empirical evidence that Sky's gifting protocol constitutively produces generosity cascades—a clear case of protocol-level prosocial design scaling to millions.

### 12. Hughes, J. (2020). Designing the Social Play of Sky: Children of the Light. *GDC 2020 Talk*. Reported via PocketTactics. URL: https://www.pockettactics.com/sky-children-of-the-light/friends

**Key contribution:** Sky's lead designer describes social play as "any activity performed near other players, which encourages simulating the thoughts and feelings of others." Details the candle-meeting ritual, heart economy, relationship progression requiring investment, and chat benches as deliberate friction to prevent toxicity.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Sky's graduated communication protocol (gesture → candle → friendship → chat) is a paradigmatic example of constitutive protocol design for prosociality. Designed friction as care.

### 13. Maj, B. A. (2022). Sky: Children of the Light – The Capitalization of Compassion. *Utrecht University Master's Thesis*. URL: https://studenttheses.uu.nl/handle/20.500.12932/41992

**Key contribution:** Textual analysis of Sky's narrative, world design, social mechanics, and microtransactions. Finds that while game mechanics promote compassion, the free-to-play revenue model eventually conflates compassionate actions with currency.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Important critical counterpoint—shows how economic protocols can undermine prosocial design. Protocol Love must account for how monetization protocols interact with prosocial ones.

### 14. Research group (2026). Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation in a Social Multiplayer Online Game: Player Influence Dynamics in Sky: Children of the Light. *ResearchGate preprint*. URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/399897690

**Key contribution:** Survey of 9,254 players + 660,000 in-game player data using COM-B model. Finds that social interaction opportunities (Opportunity) are the strongest predictor of player influence, more than capability or motivation alone.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates that designing opportunities for social interaction (protocol affordances) is more important than individual capability or motivation—protocol design matters most.

## Death Stranding (Kojima Productions)

### 15. Bostan, B., & Şengün, S. (2023). Forging Connections: The Social Dynamics of "Death Stranding" as a Paradigm Shift in Gaming. *New Trends in Computer Sciences*, 1(2), 97–112. DOI: 10.3846/ntcs.2023.19412

**Key contribution:** Close reading (148 hours) of Death Stranding's five prosocial domains: narrative infrastructure, game mechanics, player-to-player interactions, player-to-NPC interactions, and thematic resonance. Formally defines the "strand game" genre.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** The most comprehensive academic analysis of Death Stranding's prosocial architecture. Its five-domain framework maps directly onto Protocol Love's analysis of constitutive protocol layers.

### 16. Neto, A., Cardoso, P., & Carvalhais, M. (2021). Asynchronous Interactions Between Players and Game World. In *Videogame Sciences and Arts* (pp. 211–225). Springer. DOI: 10.1007/978-3-030-61671-7_14

**Key contribution:** Builds on Ian Bogost's characteristics of asynchronous play to describe dynamics achieved through asynchronous interaction. Uses Death Stranding as a primary case study for a potential new genre.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides theoretical vocabulary for analyzing asynchronous prosocial protocols—the "strand" in strand games as a protocol pattern for care-at-a-distance.

### 17. Ivanescu, A. (2023). [Death Stranding: Connection and Asynchronous Multiplayer]. *Brunel University*. URL: https://bura.brunel.ac.uk/bitstream/2438/27903/3/FullText.pdf

**Key contribution:** Analyzes Death Stranding's three levels of connection: ludic (connecting locations), asynchronous (player-to-player via structures/signs), and personal (Sam's trauma). Draws parallels to cyber-utopianism and questions whether designed connection protocols can overcome isolation.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Articulates the multi-layered nature of prosocial protocol design: narrative, mechanical, and infrastructural protocols working in concert.

### 18. Green, A. M. (2022). *Longing, Ruin, and Connection in Hideo Kojima's Death Stranding*. Routledge Advances in Game Studies. New York: Routledge.

**Key contribution:** Book-length analysis of Death Stranding's themes of connection, isolation, and rebuilding. Examines how the game's "like" system and infrastructure-building mechanics create community without direct interaction.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Scholarly treatment of how infrastructure protocols (roads, bridges, ziplines) constitute prosocial worlding—building shared worlds through material contribution.

## Dark Souls / FromSoftware (Asynchronous Message Systems)

### 19. Welsh, T. (2020). (Re)Mastering Dark Souls. *Game Studies*, 20(4). URL: http://gamestudies.org/2004/articles/welsh

**Key contribution:** Argues Dark Souls is designed to integrate players into community through tip-sharing and cooperative mechanics (Orange Guidance Soapstone, summoning). Analyzes how the game embraces "jolly cooperation" while resisting objectivization through shared collective mastery.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** The soapstone message system exemplifies constrained-vocabulary protocol design for prosociality. Template-based communication prevents toxicity while enabling meaningful help.

### 20. Mühleder, P., & Roth, M. (2021). Playing Out of Bounds? Cross-Platform Community Practice in and Beyond Dark Souls. In Roth, M. et al. (eds.), *Japan's Contemporary Media Culture*. CrossAsia-eBooks. DOI: 10.11588/crossasia.971.c12889

**Key contribution:** Explores how Dark Souls' in-game communication practices (soapstone messages, "Praise the Sun") generate cross-platform community culture. Uses network traffic data to map player-to-player message interactions.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Shows how constrained in-game protocols generate emergent cultural practices that transcend the game itself. Protocol constraints as generative of communal meaning-making.

### 21. Carter, M. (2022). An Archaeological Survey of Elden Ring's Asynchronous Multiplayer. *arXiv preprint*. URL: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.10949

**Key contribution:** Archaeological methodology applied to Elden Ring's asynchronous message system. Surveys message distribution, content, and player death data in starting areas. Treats player messages as archaeological artifacts.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Methodological innovation—treating protocol-generated player traces as material culture. Demonstrates how asynchronous messaging protocols create layered, communal information landscapes.

## Kind Words (Popcannibal)

### 22. Scott, Z. (2019–2020). Kind Words: Design and Community (Developer Interviews). *GameSpot, Polygon, Game Developer*. URL: https://www.gamespot.com/articles/how-an-online-game-community-is-helping-each-other/1100-6469968/

**Key contribution:** Developer Ziba Scott describes Kind Words as "an experiment in letting people talk to each other" with designed anonymity, one-reply-per-letter constraint, and lo-fi aesthetic. Only 3% of letters reported. Over 150,000 letters from 15,000 users.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Kind Words' protocol design—anonymous, asynchronous, one-reply, no threading—constitutively produces care. The protocol prevents escalation while enabling vulnerability. A pure case of prosocial protocol design.

### 23. Duffett, M. (2023). Deep Dive: Altruism in Online Multiplayer Video Games with Kind Words and Sky: Children of the Light. *Game Developer*. URL: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/deep-dive-altruism-in-online-multiplayer-video-games-with-kind-words-and-sky-children-of-the-light

**Key contribution:** Comparative analysis of altruistic behavior in Kind Words and Sky. Identifies empathy triggers, intrinsic motivation, and reciprocity norms as key mechanisms. Notes how both games use protocol constraints (anonymity, limited communication) to facilitate rather than hinder prosocial behavior.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Directly compares two Protocol Love case studies, identifying shared protocol patterns: anonymity, constrained communication, reciprocity scaffolding.

## Cooperative Game Design Frameworks

### 24. El-Nasr, M. S., Aghabeigi, B., Milam, D., Erfani, M., Lameman, B., Maygoli, H., & Mah, S. (2010). Understanding and Evaluating Cooperative Games. *Proceedings of CHI 2010*, 253–262. DOI: 10.1145/1753326.1753363

**Key contribution:** Identifies cooperative design patterns from 14 games and proposes Cooperative Performance Metrics (CPMs). Foundational typology of cooperative mechanics: shared puzzles, complementary abilities, communication mechanics, synergies.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides the vocabulary of cooperative patterns that Protocol Love can draw on to analyze how each case study implements cooperation at the protocol level.

### 25. Zagal, J. P., Rick, J., & Hsi, I. (2006). Collaborative Games: Lessons Learned from Board Games. *Simulation & Gaming*, 37(1), 24–40. DOI: 10.1177/1046878105282279

**Key contribution:** Distinguishes competitive, cooperative, and collaborative games using game theory. Analyzes Reiner Knizia's Lord of the Rings to extract seven observations, four lessons, and three pitfalls for collaborative game design.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes the theoretical distinction between cooperation (shared interest within competition) and collaboration (fully shared fate)—relevant to analyzing how different Protocol Love case studies fall on this spectrum.

### 26. Pais, P., Gonçalves, D., et al. (2024). A Living Framework for Understanding Cooperative Games. *Proceedings of CHI 2024*. DOI: 10.1145/3613904.3641953

**Key contribution:** Comprehensive, empirically-grounded framework for cooperative game design with four major categories: Play Configuration, Cooperation Structures, Forms of Cooperation, and Cooperation Design Patterns. Builds on El-Nasr et al. (2010) with analysis of a much larger game corpus.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** The most current and comprehensive cooperative game design framework. Its pattern taxonomy can be used to formally describe the cooperative protocols in each Protocol Love case study.

### 27. Reuter, C., Wendel, V., Göbel, S., & Steinmetz, R. (2014). Game Design Patterns for Collaborative Player Interactions. *Proceedings of DiGRA 2014*. DOI: 10.26503/dl.v2014i1.639

**Key contribution:** Extracts game design patterns for collaborative player interactions from well-received games. Classifies interactions along spatial and temporal dimensions, providing a pattern library for designers.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides dimensional analysis (spatial, temporal) of cooperative patterns that maps onto Protocol Love's analysis of synchronous vs. asynchronous, co-located vs. distributed prosocial protocols.

## Prosocial Design Theory and Practice

### 28. Cook, D., et al. (2023). Kind Games: Designing for Prosocial Multiplayer. *Polaris Game Design Retreat 2022 / Lostgarden*. URL: https://lostgarden.com/2023/07/08/kind-games-designing-for-prosocial-multiplayer/

**Key contribution:** Defines "kind games" as multiplayer games intentionally designed to emphasize prosocial behavior. Proposes core values (safety, interdependence, friendship, shared purpose) and design patterns. Cites Journey, Death Stranding, and Elden Ring as examples.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** The practitioner manifesto most aligned with Protocol Love's thesis. "Kind games" identifies the design movement; Protocol Love provides the theoretical framework (constitutive protocols) to explain why these designs work.

### 29. Cook, D., et al. (2017). Game Design Patterns for Building Friendships. *Project Horseshoe 2016 / Lostgarden*. URL: https://lostgarden.com/2017/01/27/game-design-patterns-for-building-friendships/

**Key contribution:** Design checklist for friendship formation based on social psychology: proximity, frequency, reciprocity, and shared vulnerability. Translates decades of friendship research into actionable game design patterns.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Bridges social psychology of friendship with game design practice. Protocol Love can frame these "patterns" as constitutive protocols that scaffold relational development.

### 30. Cook, D., et al. (2020). Prosocial Economics for Game Design. *Project Horseshoe 2019 / Lostgarden*. URL: https://lostgarden.com/2020/01/11/prosocial-economics-for-game-design/

**Key contribution:** Investigates how economic systems in multiplayer games can support or undermine prosociality. Identifies dark patterns (zero-sum resources, pay-to-win) and prosocial economic design patterns (positive-sum resources, gift economies).  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Extends Protocol Love's analysis to economic protocols—how resource distribution rules constitutively shape prosocial or antisocial behavior in game communities.

### 31. Cook, D., et al. (2019). Social Design Practices for Human-Scale Online Games. *Project Horseshoe 2018 / Lostgarden*. URL: https://lostgarden.com/2018/12/29/social-design-practices-for-human-scale-online-games/

**Key contribution:** Advocates for small-scale MMO design that prioritizes social capital building. Proposes best practices for multiplayer games that help build meaningful social capital through intentional community design.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Argues that scale itself is a protocol parameter—smaller communities constitutively produce different social dynamics than large ones. Design for human-scale connection.

### 32. Isbister, K. (2016). *How Games Move Us: Emotion by Design*. MIT Press. ISBN: 9780262034265

**Key contribution:** Systematic examination of how games create emotional experiences through choice, flow, avatars, social play, and physical movement. Analyzes Journey, Little Big Planet, and art games. Counters the "games produce antisocial loners" narrative.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides the emotional design theory underpinning prosocial game mechanics. Games as a medium uniquely suited to creating empathy through designed interaction—the affective dimension of protocol design.

### 33. Isbister, K. (2010). Enabling Social Play: A Framework for Design and Evaluation. In *Evaluating User Experience in Games* (pp. 11–22). Springer. URL: https://www.katherineinterface.com/isbister_social_play.pdf

**Key contribution:** Framework for evaluating social play as a design value. Distinguishes types of social play (co-located, networked, competitive, cooperative) and identifies interpersonal dynamics that designers should leverage.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides evaluation criteria for assessing whether protocol designs successfully produce the social play experiences they intend.

## Cooperative Play, Empathy, and Behavioral Effects

### 34. Velez, J. A., Greitemeyer, T., Whitaker, J. L., Ewoldsen, D. R., & Bushman, B. J. (2016). Violent Video Games and Reciprocity: The Attenuating Effects of Cooperative Gameplay on Subsequent Aggression. *Communication Research*, 43(4), 447–467. DOI: 10.1177/0093650214552519

**Key contribution:** Extends Bounded Generalized Reciprocity theory to explain cooperative game effects. Playing with a helpful teammate confirmed reciprocity expectations and increased pro-socialness toward both in-group and out-group members.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides a reciprocity mechanism—cooperative protocols create expectations of mutual help that generalize beyond the game context. Protocol-level design shapes social norms.

### 35. Greitemeyer, T. (2013). Playing Video Games Cooperatively Increases Empathic Concern. *Social Psychology*, 44(6), 408–413. DOI: 10.1027/1864-9335/a000154

**Key contribution:** Experimental evidence that cooperative (vs. competitive or solo) video game play specifically increases empathic concern for others.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Isolates cooperation as the mechanism—it's not just prosocial content, but the cooperative structure (protocol) that produces empathy.

### 36. Riar, M., Morschheuser, B., Hamari, J., & Zarnekow, R. (2020). How Game Features Give Rise to Altruism and Collective Action? Implications for Cultivating Cooperation by Gamification. *HICSS 2020*. URL: https://aisel.aisnet.org/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1102&context=hicss-53

**Key contribution:** Survey of Ingress players (N=206) showing cooperative game features give rise to within-group altruism and we-intentions (collective action). Distinguishes we-intentions from I-intentions in cooperative contexts.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates that cooperative game features (protocols) produce collective intentionality—a key mechanism for prosocial worlding.

### 37. Mengel, F. (2014). Computer Games and Prosocial Behaviour. *PLoS ONE*, 9(4), e94099. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0094099

**Key contribution:** Finds that time spent playing computer games is positively correlated with cooperation in Prisoner's Dilemma, while other computer activities (social media, browsing) show no correlation. Challenges simplistic "games are antisocial" narratives.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides evidence that game interaction protocols specifically—not screen time generally—produce prosocial behavioral tendencies.

## Animal Crossing and Pandemic-Era Prosocial Gaming

### 38. Lu, Z., et al. (2021). Players' Stories and Secrets in Animal Crossing: New Horizons—Exploring Design Factors for Positive Emotions and Social Interactions in a Multiplayer Online Game. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction (CSCW)*, 5(CSCW2). DOI: 10.1145/3474711

**Key contribution:** Survey (119 players) and interviews (25) identifying social dynamics and design factors for positive interaction in Animal Crossing. Identifies four design principles: mood-improving game worlds, social activities, rewards for social interaction, and NPC narrative integration.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Animal Crossing's visiting protocol (island visits, gift exchange, shared activities) demonstrates constitutive prosocial design in a life-simulation context—protocols for neighborliness.

### 39. Cmentowski, S., & Krüger, A. (2022). Animal Crossing and COVID-19: A Qualitative Study Examining How Video Games Satisfy Basic Psychological Needs During the Pandemic. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 13, 800683. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2022.800683

**Key contribution:** Qualitative study showing Animal Crossing satisfied autonomy, relatedness, and competence needs (Self-Determination Theory) during pandemic isolation. Players used the game as a platform for social connection and emotional regulation.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates how game protocols can satisfy fundamental psychological needs for connection during crisis—prosocial worlding as infrastructure for care in extremis.

## Empathy and Game Design

### 40. Belman, J., & Flanagan, M. (2010). Designing Games to Foster Empathy. *International Journal of Cognitive Technology*, 15(1), 11–20.

**Key contribution:** Proposes design principles for games that foster empathy: priming empathetic cognition, providing access to others' emotional experiences, enabling perspective-taking, and encouraging emotional regulation. Draws on cognitive science of empathy.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides cognitive design principles that can be implemented as interaction protocols—designing the conditions under which empathy naturally emerges through play.

### 41. (2025). Empathy in Action: Cultivating Altruism Through Immersive Game Experiences. *Frontiers in Virtual Reality*. DOI: 10.3389/frvir.2025.1716138

**Key contribution:** Mixed-methods study (64 participants) of VR prosocial games. Found significant increase in altruistic attitudes but decrease in self-reported empathy, suggesting empathy and altruism can be decoupled. Highlights moral agency and reflective engagement as key prosocial outcomes.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Important nuance—prosocial game protocols may produce altruistic action without necessarily increasing empathy measures. Protocol design should target behavioral outcomes (helping) alongside affective ones (feeling).

### 42. Skaraas, S. B., Gomez, J., & Jaccheri, L. (2019). Playing with Empathy Through a Collaborative Storytelling Game. *IFIP ICEC 2019*. URL: https://inria.hal.science/hal-02128638v1/document

**Key contribution:** Reports on a serious educational game where collaborative storytelling serves as a vehicle for empathy development. Workshop trial with 12 participants.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates how narrative co-creation protocols can foster empathy—relevant to Protocol Love's analysis of how shared worlding (collaborative narrative) constitutes prosocial community.

## Additional Design Resources and Game-Specific Analyses

### 43. Jonassen, A. (2017). Designing for Couch Co-Op: 11 Game Design Guidelines Promoting Player Cooperation. *NTNU Master's Thesis*. URL: https://www.ntnu.edu/documents/139799/1279149990/17+Article+Final_alexajon_fors%C3%B8k_2017-12-08-00-02-45_TPD4505.Alexander.Jonassen.pdf

**Key contribution:** Meta-analysis of 50 industry recommendations distilled into 11 generalized guidelines for co-op game design promoting cooperation: encourage communication, design for different skill levels, elicit prosocial behavior, make co-op additive not restrictive.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Practical design guidelines that can be reframed as protocol patterns—each guideline represents a constitutive rule that shapes cooperative behavior.

### 44. Depping, A. E., & Mandryk, R. L. (2017). Cooperation and Interdependence: How Multiplayer Games Can Foster Social Connection. *CHI 2017*. DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025468

**Key contribution:** Separates cooperation from interdependence as distinct design dimensions. Shows that interdependence—where players depend on each other's unique contributions—produces stronger social connection than mere cooperation toward shared goals.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Refines the Protocol Love analysis by distinguishing between cooperation protocols (shared goals) and interdependence protocols (mutual dependence)—the latter being more constitutive of genuine social bonds.

### 45. Game Design for Empathy: A Better Understanding of Game Mechanics and Player Interactions. *University of Montreal Master's Thesis*. URL: https://umontreal.scholaris.ca/server/api/core/bitstreams/e1d77734-eec4-448c-8137-ffd0781b8e0f/content

**Key contribution:** Proposes that applying therapeutic board game design principles to digital game mechanics can enhance empathy development. Argues that positive player interactions are often by-products rather than intentional design goals.  
**Relevance to Protocol Love:** Supports Protocol Love's argument that prosociality should be designed constitutively (at the protocol level) rather than left as an emergent by-product.

## Key Themes for Protocol Love

1. **Constrained communication as prosocial protocol**: Journey's chirps, Dark Souls' soapstone templates, Kind Words' one-reply letters—limiting communication channels constitutively prevents toxicity while enabling meaningful connection.

2. **Anonymity as design feature**: Journey, Kind Words, and Sky all use anonymity to remove prejudice barriers and enable empathy before social categorization.

3. **Asynchronous helping as care infrastructure**: Death Stranding's shared structures, Dark Souls' messages, Kind Words' letters—temporal separation transforms helping from reciprocal exchange into generalized care.

4. **Graduated intimacy protocols**: Sky's candle → friendship → chat progression exemplifies how protocol design can scaffold relationship development.

5. **Positive-sum economics**: Cook's prosocial economics framework shows how resource protocols (gift economies, shared resources) constitutively produce prosocial behavior vs. zero-sum competition.

6. **Empirical validation**: Greitemeyer, Gentile, and Ferrara provide robust experimental and large-scale empirical evidence that these protocol-level designs produce measurable prosocial behavioral change.

7. **Protocol-level vs. content-level**: The literature consistently shows that it's not game narratives alone but the structural mechanics (protocols) that drive prosocial outcomes—supporting Protocol Love's core thesis.
