## 1. Foundational Philosophy: Constitutive vs. Regulative Rules

### 1.1. Searle, J.R. (1969). *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language*. Cambridge University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Introduced the foundational distinction between constitutive rules ("X counts as Y in context C") that create new forms of activity, and regulative rules that govern pre-existing behavior. Constitutive rules make possible institutional facts through collective acceptance.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** This distinction is the philosophical backbone of the paper's thesis. Protocol Love argues that prosocial outcomes can be *constituted*—not merely regulated—at the protocol level. Searle's framework provides the conceptual vocabulary for distinguishing between protocols that reactively constrain (regulative) and those that generatively enable new social forms (constitutive).
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0521096263

### 1.2. Searle, J.R. (1995). *The Construction of Social Reality*. Free Press.
- **Key contribution:** Extended the constitutive rules framework into a full theory of institutional reality. Argues that institutional facts (money, property, marriage) exist through collective acceptance of constitutive rules that assign status functions carrying deontic powers (rights, obligations, permissions).
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates how constitutive rules create entire institutional ontologies. Protocol Love can draw on this to argue that digital protocols don't merely facilitate transactions—they *constitute* new social realities with their own deontic structure.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0684831794

### 1.3. Searle, J.R. (2010). *Making the Social World: The Structure of Human Civilization*. Oxford University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Updated account introducing "Status Function Declarations" as the unified mechanism underlying all institutional facts. Argues constitutive rules always carry deontic powers and typically require language.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The concept of Status Function Declarations maps directly onto protocol design—protocols can be understood as computational declarations that assign status functions and deontic powers in digital environments.
- **DOI:** 10.1093/acprof:osobl/9780195396171.001.0001

### 1.4. Searle, J.R. (2015). Status Functions and Institutional Facts: Reply to Hindriks and Guala. *Journal of Institutional Economics*, 11(3), 507–514.
- **Key contribution:** Defends constitutive rules against reductionist accounts, arguing that only the constitutive rule approach can adequately explain institutional reality. Regulative rules alone cannot pose the right questions about how institutions create new forms of power.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Strengthens the case that Protocol Love's constitutive approach is not reducible to a set of regulative constraints—protocols genuinely create new institutional possibilities.
- **DOI:** 10.1017/S1744137414000496

### 1.5. Searle, J.R. (2018). Constitutive Rules. *Argumenta*, 4(1), 51–54.
- **Key contribution:** Concise restatement of the constitutive/regulative distinction with emphasis on how constitutive rules create "new forms of reality, with new powers" and are "the basis of human civilization."
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Directly supports the thesis that protocol design is civilizational work—constituting new forms of social reality rather than merely optimizing existing ones.
- **DOI:** 10.14275/2465-2334/20187.sea

### 1.6. Schauer, F. (2021). On the Regulative Functions of Constitutive Rules. In *Constitutive Rules* (pp. 107–119). Springer.
- **Key contribution:** Points out that constitutive rules also have regulative "overhang"—by constituting the approved way of doing things, they make alternatives less eligible, available, or permitted.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Important nuance: even constitutive protocol design has regulative effects. Designing for prosociality constitutively still shapes what is possible and what is foreclosed—a responsibility the paper must address.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/978-3-030-54116-3_6

### 1.7. Rawls, J. (1955). Two Concepts of Rules. *Philosophical Review*, 64(1), 3–32.
- **Key contribution:** Distinguished between justifying a practice and justifying an action falling under it. Anticipated the constitutive/regulative distinction by showing that the rules of practices (like promising) are logically prior to and constitutive of the actions they govern.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Supports the argument that protocol rules are practice-defining, not merely behavior-constraining. The justification of a protocol is different from the justification of actions within it.
- **DOI:** 10.2307/2182230

## 2. Protocol Theory and Digital Control

### 2.1. Galloway, A.R. (2004). *Protocol: How Control Exists after Decentralization*. MIT Press.
- **Key contribution:** Foundational text arguing that internet protocols (TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP) constitute a new form of distributed control. Protocols are not neutral infrastructure but a "diagram" of power—they enable connection while simultaneously enforcing standardization. "The founding principle of the Net is control, not freedom."
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Essential interlocutor. Galloway shows that protocols already constitute forms of power and control. Protocol Love extends this by asking: if protocols inevitably constitute social relations, can they be designed to constitute *prosocial* ones? Moves from Galloway's critical analysis to a constructive design agenda.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0262072472

### 2.2. Galloway, A.R. & Thacker, E. (2007). *The Exploit: A Theory of Networks*. University of Minnesota Press.
- **Key contribution:** Extends protocol theory to argue that networks have their own native forms of control—simultaneously centralized and dispersed. Introduces the concept of the "exploit" as the point of leverage within networked systems.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates that network protocols create specific power topologies. Protocol Love must address how prosocial protocol design navigates these inherent power dynamics rather than naively assuming neutrality.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0816650446

### 2.3. Galloway, A.R. (2012). *The Interface Effect*. Polity Press.
- **Key contribution:** Argues the interface is an "autonomous zone of aesthetic activity" that is itself constitutive—not merely a window onto pre-existing content. Interfaces are effects of cultural, technological, and political forces, and simultaneously produce those forces.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Reinforces the constitutive thesis at the interface level. Protocol design shapes not just rules of interaction but the very phenomenology of social encounter.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0745662527

## 3. Code, Law, and Regulation

### 3.1. Lessig, L. (1999). *Code and Other Laws of Cyberspace*. Basic Books.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal argument that computer code functions as a regulatory modality alongside law, social norms, and markets. "Code is law"—software architecture constrains and enables behavior as effectively as legal regulation, but without democratic deliberation. Identified four modalities of regulation: law, norms, market, and architecture (code).
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes the regulatory power of technical design. Protocol Love builds on Lessig by arguing that if code is law, then protocol design is constitutional design—and should be approached with the same normative seriousness as lawmaking.
- **URL:** https://lessig.org/product/codev2/

### 3.2. Lessig, L. (2006). *Code: Version 2.0*. Basic Books.
- **Key contribution:** Updated and expanded version incorporating the rise of Web 2.0. Reinforces that architectural choices in cyberspace are political choices, and that the values embedded in code should be subject to democratic deliberation.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Strengthens the argument for intentional, value-laden protocol design. If architectural choices are inescapably political, the question becomes not whether to embed values in protocols but *which* values.
- **URL:** https://lessig.org/product/codev2/

### 3.3. Grimmelmann, J. (2005). Regulation by Software. *Yale Law Journal*, 114(7), 1719–1758.
- **Key contribution:** Argues software is a distinctive modality of regulation (not merely a subspecies of architecture as Lessig suggests), characterized by three properties: it acts according to rules rather than standards, it is automated rather than human-enforced, and software rules cannot be ignored.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Refines the "code is law" thesis in ways critical for Protocol Love—protocols enforce rules with machine-like precision, which has implications for how constitutive protocol design handles ambiguity, context-sensitivity, and discretion.
- **URL:** https://openyls.law.yale.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/7b66c908-d4f9-4d0a-9fee-4c3d5def25b8/content

### 3.4. De Filippi, P. & Hassan, S. (2016). Blockchain Technology as a Regulatory Technology: From Code is Law to Law is Code. *First Monday*, 21(12).
- **Key contribution:** Describes the shift from "code is law" (code having the effect of law) to "law is code" (law being defined as code). Smart contracts enable contractual and legal provisions to be transposed directly into self-executing code, creating a new regulatory paradigm.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Directly relevant to the constitutive thesis. When law becomes code, constitutive rules are literally instantiated in executable protocols. This creates both opportunities (automatic prosocial enforcement) and risks (rigidity, exclusion).
- **URL:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3097430

### 3.5. Hassan, S. & De Filippi, P. (2017). The Expansion of Algorithmic Governance: From Code is Law to Law is Code. *Field Actions Science Reports*, Special Issue 17.
- **Key contribution:** Traces the expansion of algorithmic governance from Lessig's initial insight through blockchain and smart contracts. Argues blockchain creates "guarantees of execution" that fundamentally change the nature of code-based regulation.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides the genealogy of how protocol-level governance has evolved. Protocol Love can position itself as the next step: from algorithmic governance (control) to constitutive protocol design (worlding).
- **URL:** https://hal.science/hal-01700277/document

### 3.6. De Filippi, P., Mannan, M. & Reijers, W. (2024). Blockchain Technology and the Rule of Code: Regulation via Governance. *George Washington Law Review*, 92(6), 1229–1268.
- **Key contribution:** Argues blockchain challenges the rule of law through a move toward the "rule of code." Distinguishes between "regulation by code" (imposing legal responsibilities on operators) and "regulation via governance" (using legal pressure points to influence community norms). Builds on Lessig's four modes of regulation.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The "regulation via governance" pathway aligns with constitutive design—shaping the social norms and governance practices that emerge around protocols, rather than just encoding rules in code.
- **DOI:** Available at SSRN: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5086109

### 3.7. Rules as Code (RaC) Movement. Various authors and initiatives (2018–present).
- **Key contribution:** Interdisciplinary movement exploring the practice of expressing legislation and policy in machine-readable formats alongside natural language. New Zealand's Better Rules initiative (2018) was pioneering; subsequent work at MIT Computational Law Report, Stanford Journal of Blockchain Law & Policy, and others has refined the concept.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** RaC demonstrates the practical challenges of translating normative rules into computational form—precisely the challenge Protocol Love faces. Key lesson: "legislation should never be enacted in code" but computational models alongside law hold promise.
- **URL:** https://law.mit.edu/pub/governingdigitallegalsystems; https://crlp-jerseyldo.github.io/about/rulesascode

## 4. Mechanism Design and Algorithmic Governance

### 4.1. Hurwicz, L. (1972). On Informationally Decentralized Systems. In *Decision and Organization* (pp. 297–336). North-Holland.
- **Key contribution:** Founded the field of mechanism design by introducing the concept of incentive compatibility—the idea that a mechanism's rules should align participants' self-interested behavior with desired social outcomes. Mechanisms are the "rules of the game."
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Mechanism design is the formal foundation for designing protocols that produce desired collective outcomes. Protocol Love can draw on this tradition while arguing that the objectives should be prosocial worlding, not just efficiency.
- **DOI:** N/A (book chapter)

### 4.2. Myerson, R.B. (1979). Incentive Compatibility and the Bargaining Problem. *Econometrica*, 47(1), 61–74.
- **Key contribution:** Developed the revelation principle in its greatest generality—any equilibrium outcome of any mechanism can be replicated by an incentive-compatible direct mechanism where truthful reporting is optimal. Massively simplifies the design space for mechanism designers.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The revelation principle provides a theoretical foundation for thinking about what is achievable through protocol design. Understanding its limits (e.g., impossibility results) helps Protocol Love be realistic about what constitutive design can accomplish.
- **DOI:** 10.2307/1911963

### 4.3. Myerson, R.B. (1981). Optimal Auction Design. *Mathematics of Operations Research*, 6(1), 58–73.
- **Key contribution:** Applied the revelation principle to derive the optimal auction mechanism, demonstrating the power of mechanism design to derive implementable rules from first principles.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Illustrates how formal mechanism design translates abstract objectives into concrete rules—a model for how Protocol Love might derive prosocial protocols from prosocial objectives.
- **DOI:** 10.1287/moor.6.1.58

### 4.4. Nisan, N. & Ronen, A. (2001). Algorithmic Mechanism Design. *Games and Economic Behavior*, 35(1–2), 166–196.
- **Key contribution:** Founded algorithmic mechanism design by bridging computer science and mechanism design. Proposed a formal framework for studying algorithms where participants are self-interested agents, and the algorithm must incorporate payments to incentivize truthful behavior.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides the computational framework for implementing mechanism design in digital protocols. Protocol Love's constitutive protocols must be algorithmically tractable—this paper shows how to think about computational constraints on mechanism design.
- **DOI:** 10.1006/game.1999.0790

### 4.5. Nobel Prize Committee (2007). Mechanism Design Theory. Scientific Background on the Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive overview of mechanism design theory as developed by Hurwicz, Maskin, and Myerson. Explains the revelation principle, implementation theory, and the fundamental question: "What are the best rules of the game for attaining a given objective?"
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes mechanism design as the formal science of designing "rules of the game"—Protocol Love's agenda of constitutive protocol design is a direct descendant of this tradition, extended to prosocial objectives in digital environments.
- **URL:** https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/advanced-economicsciences2007.pdf

### 4.6. Roth, A.E. (2002). The Economist as Engineer: Game Theory, Experimentation, and Computation as Tools for Design Economics. *Econometrica*, 70(4), 1341–1378.
- **Key contribution:** Articulated the vision of economics as an engineering discipline focused on practical market design. Showed through the redesign of the medical residency match (NRMP) and school choice mechanisms that well-designed rules produce dramatically better outcomes than poorly designed ones.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates that rule/protocol design has enormous practical consequences for human welfare. Protocol Love extends this "economist as engineer" vision to prosocial protocol design.
- **DOI:** 10.1111/1468-0262.00335

### 4.7. Roth, A.E. (2008). What Have We Learned From Market Design? *Economic Journal*, 118(527), 285–310.
- **Key contribution:** Distilled key lessons: successful marketplaces must provide thickness (attracting participants), overcome congestion (enabling efficient consideration of alternatives), and make participation safe (incentive-compatible and strategically simple). Showed how design failures cause market unraveling.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The three requirements of successful markets (thickness, congestion management, safety) are design principles applicable to any protocol designed for collective outcomes—including prosocial ones.
- **DOI:** 10.1111/j.1468-0297.2007.02121.x

## 5. Quadratic Mechanisms and Prosocial Funding

### 5.1. Lalley, S.P. & Weyl, E.G. (2018). Quadratic Voting: How Mechanism Design Can Radicalize Democracy. *AEA Papers and Proceedings*, 108, 33–37.
- **Key contribution:** Proposed quadratic voting (QV) where individuals pay the square of the number of votes purchased. This uniquely among mechanisms induces marginal costs linear in votes, achieving welfare-optimal collective decisions that incorporate intensity of preference.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** QV is a paradigmatic example of constitutive mechanism design for prosocial outcomes—it doesn't regulate voting behavior but constitutes an entirely new form of democratic expression. Shows that mechanism design can be *radical* in its democratic ambitions.
- **DOI:** 10.1257/pandp.20181002

### 5.2. Buterin, V., Hitzig, Z. & Weyl, E.G. (2019). A Flexible Design for Funding Public Goods. *Management Science*, 65(11), 5171–5187.
- **Key contribution:** Proposed Quadratic Finance (QF), where the funding received by a public good is the square of the sum of the square roots of individual contributions. Under standard assumptions, this achieves first-best public goods provision in a decentralized, self-organizing ecosystem. Implemented as Gitcoin Grants.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** QF is perhaps the strongest existing example of constitutive protocol design for prosocial worlding—it constitutes a new form of collective funding that is optimally responsive to community needs. Directly demonstrates that protocol-level rules can generate prosocial outcomes by design, not regulation.
- **DOI:** 10.1287/mnsc.2019.3337

### 5.3. Posner, E.A. & Weyl, E.G. (2018). *Radical Markets: Uprooting Capitalism and Democracy for a Just Society*. Princeton University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Proposed radical market mechanisms including QV, Common Ownership Self-Assessed Tax (COST), and Visas Between Individuals Program (VIP) to address inequality, monopoly, and democratic dysfunction through better mechanism design.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Demonstrates the full scope of what constitutive mechanism design can aspire to—redesigning the foundational protocols of property, democracy, and immigration for more just outcomes. Shows protocol design as a form of political philosophy.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0691177502

## 6. Smart Contracts and Computational Governance

### 6.1. Szabo, N. (1994/1996). Smart Contracts: Building Blocks for Digital Markets.
- **Key contribution:** Coined the term "smart contract"—a computerized transaction protocol that executes the terms of a contract. Proposed embedding contractual clauses in hardware and software to make breach expensive. "New institutions, and new ways to formalize the relationships that make up these institutions, are now made possible by the digital revolution."
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Szabo's vision of computationally formalized relationships is a precursor to constitutive protocol design. Smart contracts don't just enforce existing agreements—they constitute new forms of institutional relationship.
- **URL:** https://www.fon.hum.uva.nl/rob/Courses/InformationInSpeech/CDROM/Literature/LOTwinterschool2006/szabo.best.vwh.net/smart_contracts_2.html

### 6.2. Szabo, N. (1997). Formalizing and Securing Relationships on Public Networks. *First Monday*, 2(9).
- **Key contribution:** Elaborated the theory of smart contracts as combining protocols with user interfaces to formalize and secure relationships over computer networks. Derived design principles from legal principles, economic theory, and secure protocols.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes the link between protocol design and relationship formalization. Protocol Love extends this from dyadic contractual relationships to constitutive prosocial relations at the community level.
- **DOI:** 10.5210/fm.v2i9.548

## 7. Institutional Technology and Blockchain Governance

### 7.1. Davidson, S., De Filippi, P. & Potts, J. (2018). Blockchains and the Economic Institutions of Capitalism. *Journal of Institutional Economics*, 14(4), 639–658.
- **Key contribution:** Argues blockchains are not just a general-purpose technology but an *institutional technology*—a new technology for making and enforcing rules that govern economic coordination. Positions blockchain as institutional evolution analogous to the firm and the market.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The concept of "institutional technology" is central to Protocol Love's argument. Protocols are technologies for constituting institutions. This paper provides the economic framework for understanding protocol design as institutional design.
- **DOI:** 10.1017/S1744137417000200

### 7.2. De Filippi, P. & Wright, A. (2018). *Blockchain and the Law: The Rule of Code*. Harvard University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive analysis of how blockchain technology creates "Lex Cryptographia"—rules administered through self-executing smart contracts and decentralized organizations. Argues this constitutes a fundamentally new regulatory paradigm.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes that blockchain protocols constitute a new legal order. Protocol Love can build on this to argue for intentional prosocial design of this new constitutional layer.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0674976429

### 7.3. De Filippi, P. & Lavayssière, X. (2020). Blockchain Technology: Toward a Decentralized Governance of Digital Platforms? In *The First Outstanding 50 Years of "Università Politecnica delle Marche"*.
- **Key contribution:** Distinguishes between on-chain governance (governance *by* the infrastructure—rules encoded in the protocol itself) and off-chain governance (governance *of* the infrastructure—social and institutional processes for deciding what rules to encode). Proposes hybrid solutions.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** This on-chain/off-chain distinction is critical for Protocol Love. Constitutive protocol design operates primarily at the on-chain level (encoding prosocial rules) but must be complemented by off-chain governance processes for legitimacy.
- **URL:** https://hal.science/hal-03098502/document

### 7.4. De Filippi, P. et al. (2024). Report on Blockchain Governance Dynamics. SSRN Working Paper.
- **Key contribution:** Comparative analysis of governance across eleven major blockchain networks. Finds an emerging "blockchain constitutionalism"—networks increasingly formalize governance rules, but actual decentralization often falls short of goals due to power consolidation.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides empirical evidence for the gap between constitutive aspirations and actual governance outcomes in protocol design. Protocol Love must address how prosocial constitutive design avoids power consolidation.
- **URL:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5112585

## 8. Commons, Ostrom, and Digital Governance

### 8.1. Ostrom, E. (1990). *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action*. Cambridge University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Demonstrated that communities can self-govern shared resources without privatization or state control, through eight design principles including clearly defined boundaries, congruence between rules and local conditions, collective-choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, minimal recognition of rights, and nested enterprises.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Ostrom's design principles are empirically validated constitutive rules for prosocial resource governance. Protocol Love can position its constitutive protocol design as a computational instantiation of Ostromian principles.
- **DOI:** 10.1017/CBO9780511807763

### 8.2. Ostrom, E. (2005). *Understanding Institutional Diversity*. Princeton University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Developed the Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework for systematically analyzing how rules, physical conditions, and community attributes interact to produce outcomes in collective action situations.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The IAD framework provides a systematic methodology for analyzing how protocol rules interact with user characteristics and technical conditions to produce (or fail to produce) prosocial outcomes.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0691122380

### 8.3. Rozas, D., Tenorio-Fornés, A., Díaz-Molina, S. & Hassan, S. (2021). When Ostrom Meets Blockchain: Exploring the Potentials of Blockchain for Commons Governance. *SAGE Open*, 11(1).
- **Key contribution:** Identified six blockchain affordances relevant to commons governance: tokenization, self-enforcement and formalization of rules, autonomous automatization, decentralization of power, increased transparency, and codification of trust. Mapped these to Ostrom's eight principles.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Directly bridges Ostrom's constitutive institutional design with blockchain protocol design. Shows how computational affordances can enable (but also constrain) prosocial governance.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/21582440211002526

### 8.4. Poux, P. & De Filippi, P. (2021). Blockchains for the Governance of Common Goods. *Proceedings of the 1st International Workshop on Distributed Infrastructure for the Common Good* (DICG '20), ACM.
- **Key contribution:** Analyzed how blockchain technology's technological guarantees (ex-ante automation and ex-post verification) can replace traditional monitoring and sanctioning requirements in commons governance.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Shows how protocol-level guarantees can constitute commons governance structures—replacing human monitoring with computational verification while preserving Ostromian principles.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/3428662.3428793

### 8.5. Van Vulpen, P. & Jansen, S. (2023). Decentralized Autonomous Organization Design for the Commons and the Common Good. *Frontiers in Blockchain*, 6.
- **Key contribution:** Created a governance framework for "common DAOs" by synthesizing 16 works on technology for commons governance. Framework covers governance structure, enabling technology, and community governance, providing an implementation of Ostrom's principles in DAOs.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides a concrete governance framework for DAO-based commons—a model for how Protocol Love's constitutive protocols might be implemented in practice.
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fbloc.2023.1287249

## 9. Artifacts, Values, and Design Politics

### 9.1. Winner, L. (1980). Do Artifacts Have Politics? *Daedalus*, 109(1), 121–136.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal argument that technical artifacts embody political qualities in two ways: (1) specific designs can be means to settle political affairs (e.g., Robert Moses's low bridges), and (2) some technologies are inherently compatible with certain political arrangements (e.g., nuclear power requiring authoritarian management).
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Establishes that protocol design is inherently political. If artifacts have politics, then protocols have politics. Protocol Love's contribution is to make this political dimension explicit and intentionally prosocial.
- **URL:** https://www.jstor.org/stable/20024652

### 9.2. Friedman, B., Kahn, P.H. Jr. & Borning, A. (2006). Value Sensitive Design and Information Systems. In *Human-Computer Interaction and Management Information Systems: Foundations* (pp. 348–372). M.E. Sharpe.
- **Key contribution:** Defined Value Sensitive Design (VSD) as a theoretically grounded approach accounting for human values throughout the design process via an integrative tripartite methodology: conceptual investigations (identifying stakeholders and values), empirical investigations (studying stakeholder responses), and technical investigations (analyzing how designs support or hinder values).
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** VSD provides the established HCI methodology for embedding values in design. Protocol Love extends VSD from system-level design to protocol-level design—from designing *artifacts* with values to designing *constitutive rules* with values.
- **URL:** https://cseweb.ucsd.edu/~goguen/courses/271/friedman04.pdf

### 9.3. Friedman, B. & Hendry, D.G. (2019). *Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination*. MIT Press.
- **Key contribution:** Definitive account of VSD including 17 methods, 10 application domains, and theoretical foundations. Argues for using "moral and technical imaginations" together in design.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides methods that could be adapted for constitutive protocol design—stakeholder analysis, value scenarios, envisioning cards, and multi-lifespan timelines are all applicable to designing protocols for prosocial worlding.
- **DOI:** N/A (book). ISBN: 978-0262039536

### 9.4. Flanagan, M., Howe, D.C. & Nissenbaum, H. (2008). Embodying Values in Technology: Theory and Practice. In *Information Technology and Moral Philosophy* (pp. 322–353). Cambridge University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Developed "Values at Play"—a four-step methodology (values discovery, values-based conflict identification, implementation and prototyping, values verification) for integrating values into game and technology design.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides a complementary methodology to VSD. The explicit focus on values *conflict* is particularly relevant—Protocol Love must address how constitutive protocols handle conflicts between prosocial values.
- **DOI:** 10.1017/CBO9780511498725.017

### 9.5. Manders-Huits, N. (2011). What Values in Design? The Challenge of Incorporating Moral Values into Design. *Science and Engineering Ethics*, 17(2), 271–287.
- **Key contribution:** Critical analysis of VSD arguing that "technology is not merely enabling, but constitutive: it shapes our practices and institutions in important ways." Challenges VSD to more rigorously address how values are identified, prioritized, and traded off.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The insight that technology is *constitutive* (not merely enabling) directly supports Protocol Love's thesis. Also highlights the normative challenges of choosing which prosocial values to embed in protocols.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s11948-010-9198-2

## 10. Generativity and Internet Architecture

### 10.1. Zittrain, J. (2006). The Generative Internet. *Harvard Law Review*, 119(7), 1974–2040.
- **Key contribution:** Introduced "generativity" as the capacity of a system to produce unanticipated change through unfiltered contributions from diverse audiences. Argued the Internet and PCs together form a "generative grid" whose openness is both its greatest strength and vulnerability.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Generativity is a property of constitutive design—generative protocols create the conditions for unanticipated prosocial emergence. Protocol Love can frame constitutive protocol design as designing for prosocial generativity.
- **DOI:** 10.2307/4093608

### 10.2. Zittrain, J. (2008). *The Future of the Internet—And How to Stop It*. Yale University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Extended the generativity framework into a full argument about the trade-offs between openness and security. Proposed a "generativity principle" to guide Internet governance: modifications should be made where they do least harm to generative possibilities.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** The generativity principle is a design principle for Protocol Love—constitutive protocols should maximize prosocial generativity while managing risks. The trade-off between openness and control is central to constitutive protocol design.
- **URL:** https://futureoftheinternet.org/

## 11. Blockchain Governance Design Patterns

### 11.1. Xu, X. et al. (2022). A Pattern Language for Blockchain Governance. *ArXiv preprint*.
- **Key contribution:** Identified 14 architectural patterns for blockchain governance across lifecycle stages, providing systematic guidance for governance design. Patterns include voting mechanisms, delegation structures, dispute resolution, and upgrade processes.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides concrete design patterns that Protocol Love can draw upon for implementing constitutive prosocial governance in blockchain-based protocols.
- **URL:** https://export.arxiv.org/pdf/2203.00268v3.pdf

### 11.2. De Filippi, P. et al. (2024). The Blockchain Governance Toolkit: A Cookbook for a Resilient and Robust Ecosystem. Project Liberty Institute & BlockchainGov.
- **Key contribution:** Practical toolkit structured around five steps: balancing governance trade-offs, selecting governance primitives, building resilience through safeguards, handling exogenous governance, and evaluating outcomes. Based on analysis of 11 leading blockchain networks.
- **Relevance to Protocol Love:** Provides a practical methodology for governance design that Protocol Love can adapt. The "cookbook" metaphor of combining governance "ingredients" aligns with constitutive protocol design as selecting and combining prosocial building blocks.
- **URL:** https://blockchaingov.eu/the-blockchain-governance-toolkit-a-cookbook-for-a-resilient-and-robust-ecosystem/

## Summary: Landscape and Gaps

This literature reveals several key themes relevant to Protocol Love:

1. **Philosophical foundation is strong:** Searle's constitutive/regulative distinction provides a rigorous conceptual framework. The extension to digital technology (Galloway, Lessig, Winner) is well-established.

2. **Mechanism design provides formal tools:** From Hurwicz through Myerson to Nisan & Ronen, the mathematical toolkit for designing rules that produce desired outcomes is mature. Quadratic mechanisms (Weyl, Buterin) show these can target prosocial goals.

3. **Empirical precedent exists:** Ostrom's commons governance, Roth's market design, and blockchain governance experiments provide evidence that constitutive rule design works in practice.

4. **The gap Protocol Love fills:** No existing work explicitly brings together (a) Searle's constitutive rules framework, (b) protocol theory (Galloway), (c) mechanism design, and (d) prosocial design values to articulate a coherent theory of *constitutive protocol design for prosocial worlding*. The literature treats these as separate traditions. Protocol Love's contribution is the synthesis and the normative argument that protocol design should be understood as constitutive worlding.

5. **Key tensions to address:**
   - Constitutive rules also have regulative overhang (Schauer)
   - Code rigidity vs. normative ambiguity (Grimmelmann, RaC)
   - Power consolidation despite decentralization claims (De Filippi et al. 2024)
   - The gap between formal mechanism design and actual human behavior
