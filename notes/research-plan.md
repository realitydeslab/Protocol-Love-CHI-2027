## Research Questions

### RQ1: What constitutive protocol strategies do prosocial digital worlds employ to produce care, reciprocity, and cooperation?
- **Scope:** Identify and taxonomize the specific design moves (not just features, but structural protocol decisions) that constitute prosociality in the case study systems.
- **Method:** Qualitative comparative case analysis of Journey, Sky: Children of the Light, Death Stranding, Kind Words, and Dark Souls (messages/summon signs).
- **Expected output:** A typology of constitutive prosocial protocol strategies (e.g., subtractive constitution, reciprocal entanglement, gift economies, low-bandwidth expressivity, temporal protocols, repair-oriented architectures).

### RQ2: How does constitutive protocol design differ from regulative governance (moderation, sanctions, nudges) in producing prosocial outcomes?
- **Scope:** Clarify the theoretical distinction between designing prosociality *into* the protocol vs. managing behavior *after* the protocol is set.
- **Method:** Conceptual analysis drawing on Searle's constitutive/regulative distinction, Lessig's code-as-law, Galloway's protocol theory, and Tronto's care ethics.
- **Expected output:** A conceptual framework (the "Protocol Love" framework) that articulates when and why constitutive approaches produce different outcomes than regulative ones.

### RQ3: What are the failure modes and limitations of constitutive prosocial protocol design?
- **Scope:** Identify where constitutive design can go wrong — paternalism, exclusion, over-constraint, the "regulative overhang" of constitutive rules (Schauer 2021).
- **Method:** Critical analysis of the case studies' limitations, supplemented by Galloway's critique of protocol power and feminist HCI's attention to exclusion.
- **Expected output:** A critical discussion of failure modes and design tensions, making the paper honest about limitations.

### RQ4: How can the Protocol Love framework inform the design of prosocial agentic/AI systems?
- **Scope:** Extend the framework beyond games to emerging agentic systems (AI agents, multi-agent platforms) where protocol design will increasingly shape social outcomes.
- **Method:** Speculative design analysis — applying the constitutive protocol framework to emerging domains.
- **Expected output:** A forward-looking discussion section connecting Protocol Love to AI alignment, agentic web design, and Amber's broader research on agent ethology.

## Methodology

**Approach:** Qualitative comparative case study with conceptual framework development.

1. **Case Selection:** 5 primary cases (Journey, Sky, Death Stranding, Kind Words, Dark Souls) selected for maximum variation in genre, scale, and platform while sharing constitutive prosocial design. 2-3 secondary cases (Be My Eyes, Wikipedia talk pages, Signal defaults) to extend beyond games.

2. **Data Sources:**
   - Designer talks and postmortems (GDC, blogs, interviews)
   - Published academic studies of each system
   - Direct gameplay/interaction analysis (autoethnographic)
   - Published player/user accounts and community analysis

3. **Analysis:**
   - Open coding of design decisions that constitute prosocial outcomes
   - Axial coding to identify recurring strategies across cases
   - Theoretical coding using Searle's constitutive rules, Lessig's four modalities, and Tronto's care ethics
   - Comparison with regulative approaches (moderation, sanctions) to sharpen the constitutive/regulative distinction

4. **Framework Development:**
   - Synthesize findings into the Protocol Love framework
   - Validate against additional cases (secondary cases, reviewer suggestions)
   - Articulate design implications and failure modes

---

## Contributions

1. **Conceptual framework:** The Protocol Love framework — a vocabulary for analyzing and designing constitutive prosocial protocols, grounded in Searle's constitutive rules and care ethics.

2. **Typology:** A taxonomy of constitutive prosocial protocol strategies derived from cross-case analysis (subtractive constitution, reciprocal entanglement, gift economies, low-bandwidth expressivity, temporal protocols, repair-oriented architectures).

3. **Theoretical synthesis:** Bridges previously disconnected literatures — prosocial game design practice (Cook et al.), protocol theory (Galloway, Lessig), care ethics (Tronto, Held), restorative justice (Schoenebeck), and values in design (Flanagan, Friedman).

4. **Design implications:** Actionable guidance for HCI researchers and designers building systems that constitute prosociality rather than merely moderating antisociality.

5. **Forward-looking framing:** Extends the framework to agentic/AI systems, connecting to Amber's broader research program on agent ethology and more-than-human design.

## Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| CHI 2027 deadline shifts earlier | Start writing early (Phase 1 includes RW drafting). CSCW rolling deadline as backup. |
| "Too theoretical, not enough empirical" reviewer critique | Strengthen case analyses with specific design examples. Consider adding a small user study or expert interview component. |
| Overlap with Kind Games (Cook et al.) | Explicitly position against Cook et al. — they provide design patterns, we provide theoretical framework. Ideally collaborate with Cook to avoid duplication. |
| Scope creep (too many case studies, too many literatures) | Stick to 5 primary cases. Secondary cases in discussion only. Focus literature on the 10 closest papers identified in related works. |
| "Constitutive/regulative distinction is not new" (Searle critique) | Acknowledge Searle's long history; argue the *application* to digital protocol design is novel. Cite Schauer (2021) on regulative overhang to show engagement with philosophical nuance. |
